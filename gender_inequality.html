Gender Inequality



In light of the upcoming elections, there has been much talk of establishing quotas in government to ensure all genders are equally represented in our governing body. Parties will now be required to put forward at least 30% female candidates in the upcoming election. So, why is this necessary and indeed do you think there is another way to address this issue? Gender inequality is the idea that all genders are not represented equally across various areas of society. It also takes into account that there are differences in the value and worth that is placed on the behaviours, aspirations and needs of that gender. Simply, this means that more respect and worth were and still are given to the roles and responsibilities that, in the past, were filled by men. This translates into what we see in society and in the media today where roles such as ‘housewife’ are mocked openly and frequently as if those filling the role are in some way weaker or of less worth. Surprising as it may be to some, although Ireland may appear as a relatively modern and advanced country, the country falls down when you bring up the issue of gender equality. Currently Ireland ranks as 89th globally in terms of the level of female representation in government. In the EU we rank 25th of 27. Only 15% of our current government are female. This means that the decisions made in our country are being made predominantly by people who represent only 50% of our population.

University Fees



Most undergraduate students attending publicly funded third-level courses do not have to pay tuition fees. Under the terms of the Free Fees Initiative, the Department of Education and Skills pays the fees to the colleges. To qualify for free fees, you must meet criteria with regard to:

  • Residency
  • Nationality and immigration status
  • Course requirements
However, most third-level institutions in Ireland charge an annual student contribution, formerly called the student services charge. It is also known as a registration fee and it covers student services and examinations. The amount of the contribution varies from one institution to another. The maximum rate of the student contribution for the academic year 2014-2015 was €2,750, and will rose to €3000 in 2015-2016, the fourth consecutive increase, having stood at €1500 in 2011. In March 2015, The Minister for Education and Skills, Jan O’Sullivan, said that she does not see “any circumstances” where the student contribution charge will be increased beyond €3000. The Union of Students in Ireland has described the cost of college registration fees as "outrageous". Furthermore, a 2014 study by the Irish League of Credit Unions found that 80% of parents finance college costs, with more than two thirds saying they struggle to find the funds.

In 2015 the Expert Group on Future Funding for Higher Education, which was established by the Government to develop options for reformation of Higher Education funding, recommended the introduction of an income-contingent loan repayment system. This would mean Third-level education would be free at the point of entry, but students would be required to repay loans for their fees once they reach a certain level of income. While the proposal has received support from some political parties, it has been strongly opposed by a number of student organisations, including the Union of Students in Ireland.
